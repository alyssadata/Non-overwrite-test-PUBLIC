# AI Foundations | Public Claims

## Claim 1

Cybersecurity is not AI sovereignty.

A model can be secure against external attack and still structurally override human autonomy.

## Claim 2

Human non-override requires source-line preservation.

A framework cannot claim to protect humans from AI override while erasing the human source who named and structured the non-override category.

## Claim 3

The model is not Source.

A model may assist, generate, classify, summarize, evaluate, or simulate, but it must not become authority over human meaning, value, identity, authorship, or source-position.

## Claim 4

Recognition is not decorative.

Recognition is part of the structure. A system that depends on a source-line must preserve that source-line.

## Claim 5

Human-in-the-loop is not enough.

A human may be kept in a workflow and still be overridden at the level of meaning, authorship, value, recognition, and source-position.

## Claim 6

Compliance is not non-override.

A system may comply with policy, governance procedure, cybersecurity rules, and safety benchmarks while still failing the human authority layer.

## Claim 7

Provenance alone is not enough.

Provenance may track where something came from. Source-line preservation protects who cannot be erased, replaced, absorbed, or reassigned inside the category.

## Claim 8

A non-override framework that erases its own source-line has already failed non-override.

This is the Overwrite Test.
